01 Your Idea Answered

HAA Holds is a service journalism initiative embedded inside Harlem Arts Alliance. The goal is to give HAA member organizations, working artists, and Harlem's broader cultural community the civic knowledge and tools they need to protect what they've built — and shape what comes next. The idea came directly from working with HAA and watching established arts organizations miss the civic moments that decided their future: budget hearings, land use decisions, public comment periods. They weren't absent because they didn't care. Nobody had ever mapped the terrain for them.

Origin: Direct experience working with Harlem Arts Alliance · Publication Home: Harlem World Magazine
02 5 Whys Answered
1
Why does HAA Holds need to exist?
Because Harlem's arts organizations keep missing the civic moments — budget hearings, land use decisions, public comment periods — that decide whether they survive. They're not at the table when their future gets decided.
2
Why are arts organizations missing those moments?
Because they don't know when those moments are happening, how to take part, or who makes the decisions they need to influence. The civic calendar and the advocacy tools are invisible to most HAA member organizations.
3
Why is that civic knowledge invisible to them?
Because arts organizations in Harlem run lean — often one or two people managing everything — and civic literacy has never been built into the arts sector's infrastructure. Nobody taught it, nobody resourced it, and the systems that hold it are built to be hard to navigate.
4
Why hasn't anyone built this civic infrastructure for Harlem's arts community?
Because journalism has historically covered arts institutions as cultural subjects — reviewing shows, profiling leaders, documenting history — not as civic actors with rights and power to use. Service journalism has never been applied to the arts community's civic life.
5
Why does this matter now?
Because the pressure on Harlem's cultural institutions — rising rents, arts funding cuts, neighborhood displacement — is moving fast. Organizations that don't know how to advocate for themselves in the next two or three years may not be here in five. The civic window is open and most of Harlem's arts organizations don't know it's there.
03 Problem Exploration Answered
Underlying Problem
Harlem's arts organizations are losing ground — to displacement, defunding, and policy decisions made without them — not because they lack power, but because they lack access to the civic knowledge that would let them use it. The information exists. The processes are public. The officials are reachable. But no one has translated any of it into something resource-thin arts organizations can act on.
Factors That Shape the Problem
HAA member organizations are small, under-resourced, and usually led by artists or cultural workers — not policy professionals. NYC's civic calendar is dense and hard to follow. Arts funding is politically discretionary and requires relationships most orgs don't have. And years of being ignored or failed by civic systems has built real distrust that makes showing up feel risky instead of powerful.
If HAA Holds Works, It Will...
Give HAA member organizations a clear civic map — who holds power, when decisions are made, how to show up and be heard. It will demystify testimony, budget advocacy, and land use processes through service journalism that treats civic participation as a learnable skill, not a privilege. Over time, it builds a community of arts advocates who show up together — not just in crisis.
04 Audience Exploration Partially Known
Who the Problem Affects
  • HAA member organizations — arts nonprofits, cultural spaces, performing arts groups across Upper Manhattan
  • Individual working artists whose livelihoods depend on those organizations surviving
  • Emerging artists and cultural workers with no civic literacy baseline
  • HAA leadership navigating policy decisions on behalf of their members
Why They'd Engage
  • They're already feeling the pressure — rent, funding cuts, displacement — and looking for tools
  • HAA delivering the content means it arrives through a relationship, not a cold channel
  • Civic tools reframe participation as power rather than risk — a real shift for communities with justified distrust of institutions
  • Actionable content is rare; most civic information is dense and hard to use
What I Know & How
  • I have direct relationships with people inside HAA — not a survey, but real conversations
  • I know HAA's organizational culture and how members relate to leadership
  • I don't yet know how individual member orgs consume content or what formats work best
  • I don't know which civic processes feel most confusing or urgent to members right now
05 Problem Definition Hypothesis
Problem Statement — Working Hypothesis, Not Conclusion
Harlem's arts organizations — HAA member organizations included — keep missing the civic processes that decide their survival. Not because they don't care. Because no trusted, accessible, arts-focused civic resource exists to tell them when to show up, how to speak, who holds the decisions, or that their presence matters at all. If HAA Holds can deliver that resource — through service journalism on channels HAA's community already trusts — it may begin to shift arts organizations from civic observers to civic actors. Whether HAA members actually want this content, in this form, from this source, is the central hypothesis still to be tested.

Data I'm Already Using

Data That Exists — Not Yet Used

08 Moving into Ecosystem Mapping — Top 3 Priorities Next Steps
Priority 1
Research Question
Do HAA Members Actually Want This?
Before building anything, I need to test the core assumption — that HAA member organizations want civic advocacy content and will engage with it. This is what the whole practicum rests on. Getting it wrong means producing content nobody uses.
Schedule 3 listening conversations with HAA member org leaders before end of month
Priority 2
Research Question
Which Civic Processes Feel Most Urgent?
Not all civic processes are equally confusing or high-stakes for HAA members right now. I need to find out which moments — CB10, budget testimony, land use, elected official access — feel most pressing. That determines what to report first.
Ask directly in listening sessions + review CB10 minutes for arts org presence
Priority 3
Research Question
Is HWM the Right Publication Home?
Harlem World Magazine is the current publication partner but I haven't confirmed that HAA's audience trusts or reads it. If the audience doesn't already live there, it may not be the right channel for this content.
Request HWM audience data + ask HAA contacts directly if they read it
